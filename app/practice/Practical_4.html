Lesson #4. Conditions requiring first aid. Respiratory and circulatory arrest, measures to revive the body

1. Definition of the concept of "first aid". General principles of providing first aid to victims in emergency situations.

First aid is a set of simple measures carried out directly at the site of injury or near it as a form of self- and mutual assistance, as well as by participants in emergency rescue operations using standard and improvised means.

When providing first aid, the following principles should be followed:

  1. Correctness and appropriateness
  2. Speed and care
  3. Determination and calmness.

First aid measures:

  • performing cardiopulmonary resuscitation;
  • temporary stopping of external bleeding;
  • anesthesia;
  • immobilization of damaged limbs;
  • application of aseptic dressings to wounds.

Assessing the condition of the victim is an assessment of the vital functions of the body (Fig. 14).

Fig. 14. Evaluation of vital signs of the victim's body

The procedure for assessing consciousness:

1. Approach the victim.

2. Holding his head in place with your hand, shake him by the shoulder.

3. Ask questions: Do you need help? Can you hear me? What happened? What hurts?

Algorithm for determining the state of consciousness:

A – Alert - consciousness preserved

V – Voice - responds to voice

P – Pain - reacts to pain

U – Unresponsive - no reaction

To carry out a breathing assessment it is necessary:

1. Listen to the breathing at the mouth.

2. Check for respiratory movements of the chest and abdomen.

To assess blood circulation, it is necessary to feel the pulse in the carotid artery.

The concept of clinical death

At present, it can be considered established that death (dying) is not only a qualitative “leap” from the living state of an organism to the dead, but also a more or less prolonged biological process consisting of several phases.

The following medical and biological phases are distinguished, which were one of the first to be described by the prominent Russian resuscitation physician, academician V. A. Negovsky :

1) pre-mortem, or pre-agonistic , phase;

2) terminal stop phase;

3) the agony phase;

4) clinical death;

5) biological death.

The pre-death or pre-agonic phase may have different durations in different cases. If, for example, as a result of an electric shock the work of the human heart immediately stops, then the pre-death phase is practically absent.

In many other cases, when a person dies due to bleeding, traumatic shock, or lung failure, this phase can take several hours.

Transition from the dying stage ( pre-agonistic ) phases to agony occurs through a very short phase called terminal arrest: the dying person's breathing stops and reflexes fade. The terminal arrest phase lasts from five seconds to four minutes.

After the terminal stop comes agony , at the beginning of which there is usually an increase in all weakened, "extinguished" functions of the body. Some researchers believe that the brain of a dying person, making a final effort, mobilizes its capabilities, so consciousness, thinking, memory and other cognitive processes return to it, becoming "brighter" than usual.

Already in the agony phase, when the heart and respiratory system are still working, the patient loses consciousness. In an unconscious state, the dying person no longer reacts even to strong stimuli. In this state, the patient makes chaotic and automated movements (periodically hits himself on the chest and stomach, bends the edge of the sheet with his fingers, etc.), since only the subcortical formations and the vital centers of the medulla oblongata, freed from the control of the cortex, act in him, and this is in conditions of deepening oxygen starvation.

During the agony phase, the activity of the medulla oblongata is preserved, and as a result, so is the breathing and work of the cardiovascular system of the dying person. As a result of overstimulation of the respiratory center of the brain located here, the breathing of the dying person becomes deep and spasmodic.

As oxygen starvation deepens further, the hyperactivity of the medulla oblongata is replaced by inhibition, and after some time breathing stops.

During the agony, the heart's function is also disrupted, and eventually it stops beating.

Thus, the phase of clinical death begins from the moment when the work of the heart and respiratory system stops. The duration of this phase, if we average the estimates of different authors, is 4-7 minutes. This is a short phase between life and death .

However, there are significant individual differences in the duration of the clinical phase and the rate of death among people, so resuscitation efforts should be started even in cases where more time than the specified 4–7 minutes has passed since the onset of the clinical death phase.

Many facts show that the clinical phase in children reaches 8-10 minutes and more. In some cases, even children who were in the clinical phase for up to 25-30 minutes were saved. In any case, when healthy children, as a result of accidents, find themselves in the phase of clinical death, then this phase takes them much longer than in elderly and sick people.

The last (final) stage of the existence of a once living organism is considered to be biological death, in which irreversible changes and complete cessation of all life processes occur. Its signs are: the appearance of hypostatic (cadaveric) spots, a cold body, rigor mortis.

3. Sequence of measures to revive the body. Measures to restore and maintain airway patency. Artificial respiration using the mouth-to-mouth method, closed cardiac massage

Resuscitation (from lat. re — prefix expressing: renewal, repetition + lat. animator — life-giving ), a set of measures to revive a person in a state clinical death .

In a medical sense, resuscitation may include cardiopulmonary resuscitation (Fig.15) , intensive therapy and a range of measures aimed at maintaining life.

Before starting resuscitation measures, it is necessary to lay the victim on a hard surface, unbutton his clothes as much as possible, and loosen his tie and belt.

Fig. 15. Stages of cardiopulmonary resuscitation

Stage C. Carrying out closed (indirect) cardiac massage.

Conditions for performing closed cardiac massage (Fig. 16):

1. Pressure is applied to the sternum 2-3 cm above the xiphoid process.

2. The depth of the sternum bend is 4 cm.

3. The frequency of chest compressions is 90-100 per minute.

The ventilation-massage ratio is 2:30.

Fig. 16. Closed cardiac massage

It is generally accepted that the basis of indirect cardiac massage is the compression of the heart from the front by the sternum, from behind - by the spinal column, as a result of which blood from the heart cavities enters the vessels of the body. This is the so-called cardiac pump . But during indirect cardiac massage, not only the heart is compressed, but also other intrathoracic structures (the most important is the compression of the lungs, which contain a significant amount of blood and are easily compressed). This mechanism is commonly called the chest pump .

During closed cardiac massage, both mechanisms work in a person, but the chest pump predominates, when, when the chest is compressed, the movement of blood into the aorta is ensured by the compression of all vascular capacities. The main capacity is the lungs with their pulmonary circulation.

Stage A. Ensuring airway patency:

This is the first resuscitation measure, which largely ensures the success of the entire cardiopulmonary resuscitation. The causes of airway obstruction are tongue retraction, obstruction by foreign bodies (blood, vomit, mucus, laryngospasm, bronchospasm). The most common cause is tongue retraction to the back wall of the pharynx in an unconscious patient . This is explained by the inevitable loss of tone in the muscles of the lower jaw and neck, which raise the root of the tongue above the back wall of the pharynx. Thus, the tongue, due to its own weight, retracts to the back wall of the pharynx and acts as a valve during inhalation, preventing the flow of air.

Airway obstruction can be partial or complete.

In case of partial obstruction, noisy breathing is heard, and accessory muscles (retraction of intercostal spaces, neck muscles) participate in the act of breathing.

An attempt to place a pillow under the patient's head leads to the transition of partial obstruction to complete (Fig. 17). With complete obstruction of the airways, active but unsuccessful attempts to inhale are noted, which after a few minutes are replaced by respiratory arrest, and then cardiac arrest.

Fig. 17. Obturation of the airways with the tongue

Thus, obstruction of the airways, regardless of the degree of obstruction and the cause that caused it, can cause the death of the patient.

The main method of opening the airways is the triple Safar airway maneuver, which consists of tilting the head back, pushing the lower jaw forward, and opening the mouth (Fig. 18).

Fig. 18. Restoring airway patency

After performing the triple Safar maneuver on the airways (it takes a few seconds to perform), it is necessary to take 3-5 breaths into the victim's lungs. If the chest does not inflate, one can suspect obstruction of the airways by a foreign body.

Foreign substances can be removed with the fingers (Fig. 19). To do this, insert the index finger deep into the pharynx to the base of the tongue, trying to use the finger, like a hook, to remove the foreign body. It is important not to push it further into the respiratory tract. You can use the index and middle fingers for this purpose, manipulating them like tweezers.

Fig. 19. Removal of foreign bodies from the oral cavity

Stage B. Performing artificial respiration. There are two main methods of artificial respiration : mouth-to-mouth and mouth-to-nose (Fig. 20).

The mouth-to-mouth method. The essence of the method is that the person providing assistance blows air from his lungs into the victim's lungs through his mouth. Artificial respiration "mouth-to-mouth" is more rational than other methods of artificial ventilation of the lungs, since the exhaled air of a healthy person contains 4% carbon dioxide, which is a stimulant of the respiratory center of the victim's brain, and 16% oxygen, sufficient to maintain his life. In addition, atmospheric air, located in the upper respiratory tract and containing 21% oxygen, joins the exhaled air.

Artificial respiration "from mouth to mouth" is dangerous due to infection, therefore artificial ventilation of the lungs must be carried out using gauze, a napkin or a handkerchief.

Artificial mouth-to-nose breathing It is performed if the victim has clenched teeth or has an injury to the lips or jaws. 

When performing artificial respiration, it is important to remember that the blowing should be quick and sharp.

Lesson 2. Ensuring safety and the procedure for citizens to follow in the event of fires in buildings

1. The main causes of fires at industrial and civil facilities. Dangerous fire factors

It is considered a fire an uncontrolled fire outside a designated firebox that causes or may cause damage.

To fires in housing stock fires are related in residential premises intended or actually used for the residence of citizens, including fires in buildings and structures adjacent to them (bathhouses, sheds, garages, etc.).

The term “major fires” shall be understood as a fire, the damage from which amounts to 250 or more times the amount of the base value established on the day the fire occurred.

Damage caused by fires consists of the cost of destroyed or damaged fixed and working capital of organizations, unfinished capital construction and repair projects, and property of citizens.

Those killed in fires include persons who died at the scene of the incident or within seven days from the date of receiving bodily injuries from exposure to fire, high temperatures of combustion products and smoke, as a result of the collapse of structures and other dangerous factors of fire.

Conditions that promote fires in housing stock. The increase in the number of fires and other emergency situations at the present stage is objectively facilitated by a number of conditions associated with human life.

The housing stock is deteriorating, stove heating and electrical wiring are aging and not being updated for a long time, which is what causes their emergency condition. In 20-25% of cases, the cause of fires that occur in the housing stock are faulty electric heaters, electrical and gas household appliances that have exhausted their service life, and violation of the rules for their use.

Fire hazards (FH) - fire factors , the impact of which leads to injury , poisoning or death of a person, as well as to material damage . These include:

  • Flame, spark and high temperature. The combustion of all liquid, gaseous and most solid combustible substances that, when decomposing or evaporating, release gaseous products, is accompanied by the formation of a flame.

When technological installations burn, the intensity of heat radiation is so great that a person without special protective equipment cannot approach them closer than 10 m.

The flame temperature during combustion in air of some flammable substances is: wood - 850-1400°C, petroleum products in a tank - 1100-1300°C, carbon disulfide - 2195°C. At the same time, the temperature threshold for the viability of human tissue is 45°C.

Inhalation of heated air leads to damage and necrosis of the upper respiratory tract, suffocation and death of a person. When exposed to temperatures above 100°C, a person loses consciousness and dies within a few minutes.

Skin burns are dangerous for humans. The time it takes for a person to get second-degree burns is short: at an ambient temperature of 71°C - 26 sec, 15 sec. - at 100°C. Thus, a high ambient temperature is dangerous to human life not only in a burning room, but also in adjacent rooms where combustion products and heated air have entered.

  • Smoke. Smoke consists mainly of water vapor, gases formed during combustion, and many tiny solid unburned particles (coal, resinous products, etc.). Smoke is dangerous not only because of the toxic substances it contains, but also because it reduces visibility. Evacuation in such conditions becomes difficult or impossible.
  • Toxic combustion products. Combustion products often contain 50-100 types of chemical compounds that have a toxic effect - for example, when linoleum burns, hydrogen sulfide and sulfur dioxide are released; when upholstered furniture containing polyurethane foam burns, hydrogen cyanide is released. But the main cause of death in fires is carbon monoxide poisoning (CO). Carbon monoxide poisoning accounts for 18-25% of fatal intoxication cases.
  • One of the dangerous factors of a fire is a low oxygen content, since the combustion process occurs with intensive absorption of oxygen. Therefore, oxygen starvation may occur in fire conditions. Under normal conditions, a person breathes atmospheric air with an oxygen content of 20.9%. When the oxygen content in the air is 16-18%, there is an increased heart rate, a slight disorder of motor coordination, and a slight decrease in thinking ability. At 9% oxygen content in the breathing zone, loss of consciousness occurs, at 6% - death in minutes.

About 73% of those killed in fires die from exposure to toxic combustion products, about 20% from exposure to high temperatures, and about 5% from low oxygen levels. The rest die from injuries sustained as a result of collapsed building structures, flying debris from explosions, etc.

2. Procedure for actions of managers, officials, employees and citizens in the event of a fire in industrial and civil buildings

The main functions of the manager upon detection of a fire at a facility:

1. Reports an emergency to a higher authority, analyzes the situation, makes a decision.

2. Organizes the evacuation of people and material assets from the danger zone.

3. Manages fire extinguishing until emergency rescue units arrive.

The main functions of an employee upon detection of a fire at a facility:

1. Notifies the fire department and the facility manager about the fire.

2. Begins to extinguish the fire or leaves the danger zone if there is a threat to life and health.

Actions of a citizen who discovers a fire in a building:

1. Reports a fire to the fire department, alerts people and takes measures to evacuate them.

2. Takes measures to extinguish the fire.

Control over the current situation at the facility during a fire will depend not only on the manager or employee of the facility, but also on representatives of the volunteer fire brigade (VFB).

Correct actions of VFB representatives upon detection of a fire at a facility:

When a fire breaks out, the commander of a volunteer fire brigade takes measures to extinguish it, leads the volunteers and workers of the workshop, the section for extinguishing it, and, if necessary, gives the command to evacuate workers and employees. When the maximum permissible concentration of smoke is reached, he gives the command to evacuate the persons involved in localizing the fire.

Upon arrival, the shop manager, facility manager or fire service personnel report the situation and are placed at their disposal.

Vigilante №1. In case of a fire, notifies the fire department by calling 101 and notifies the management of the enterprise and the guard at the checkpoint. Meets the fire department and indicates the location of the fire. In the absence of the head of the VPD, performs his duties.

Vigilante №2. In the event of a fire, takes part in the evacuation of workers and employees, works with a fire nozzle from an internal fire hydrant, a fire extinguisher or other fire extinguishing means. In the absence of the head of the VFB, performs the duties of Vigilante №1.

Vigilantes №3 and №4. In case of fire, they ensure the evacuation of people from the premises. They extinguish the fire with the available fire extinguishing means. When the maximum smoke concentration is reached, they evacuate the building.

3. Features of behavior during a fire in multi-story buildings. Rules for evacuating people during a fire. Safety measures when in smoke-filled rooms

Signs of a fire starting. Most often, a fire in residential buildings starts with the appearance of a small flame, which is preceded by a more or less prolonged period of heating or smoldering of solid flammable objects, materials, substances.

The presence of a smell of overheated substance and the appearance of light, at first barely noticeable, and then increasingly thickening and affecting the eyes smoke are the first sure signs of a fire. Electric wires, gradually heating up when overloaded, first signal this with a characteristic smell of rubber, and then the insulation ignites and burns or smolders, setting fire to nearby objects, wooden building structures.

Along with the smell of rubber, the light may go out or the electric lamps may begin to glow dimly, which is sometimes also a sign of impending ignition of the insulation of electrical wires.

When the room where the fire started has increased ventilation (open window, door to the balcony), people in the neighboring rooms sometimes learn about the fire not by smoke or the smell of burning, but by the crackling of burning wood. Sometimes a whistling sound is heard, and flame reflections may be visible.

What to do if you are caught in a fire in a multi-story building (apartment building, hotel, etc.) First of all, when entering any unfamiliar building, try to remember your way, pay attention to the location of the main and emergency exits.

1. If you hear cries of “Fire!” or smell smoke, or see flames, call the Ministry of Emergency Situations on duty at “101”.

2. Try to stay calm and calm the people around you. Find out where it is coming from, then start moving towards the exit. When moving in a crowd, let children, women and the elderly go first. Help those who are paralyzed by fear and cannot move, talk to them calmly and clearly, support them by the arms.

3. When you find yourself in a crowd, bend your elbows and press them to your sides, clenching your fists. Lean your body back, pushing your feet forward, and try to hold back the pressure with your back, freeing up space in front and moving slowly. Shield children with your back or sit them on your shoulders.

4. Do not enter where there is a high concentration of smoke! Modern buildings contain a lot of plastic and synthetics, which emit highly toxic substances when burning. A few breaths can lead to death.

5. When rooms and corridors are filled with smoke, go towards the smoke-free staircase or the exit, but not towards the elevator. Hold on to the walls, handrails, breathe through a handkerchief or clothing. If the concentration of smoke increases, bend down or crawl.

6. If you cannot go out onto the stairs or into the hallway due to thick smoke, high temperatures and fire, you must immediately return back, closing the door tightly behind you. And plug the cracks and ventilation holes with wet rags. Create a supply of water in the bathroom.

7. If a dangerous concentration of smoke and elevated temperature is formed in the apartment (room), you should go out onto the balcony or loggia, closing the door tightly. Take a wet blanket, carpet, or other thick fabric with you to cover yourself from the fire if it penetrates through the door or window openings.

8. If it is dangerous to remain in the room or on the balcony, try to go down to the floor below, using tightly tied sheets, curtains, ropes, etc. You can also use a fire hose for self-rescue. We recommend rescuing one at a time, backing each other up.

9. If you decide to escape through a heavily smoke-filled corridor, which is extremely dangerous, we advise you to take a wet thick cloth, which you should cover yourself with, and move bent over or crawling. If a shaft of fire is approaching you, then, without delay, fall, covering your head with a cloth, at this moment do not breathe, so as not to get burns to your internal organs.

4. Purpose of technical means of fire protection. Primary fire extinguishing means

Technical fire protection equipment is used in cases where, due to production technology conditions, it is impossible to exclude the possibility of contact between flammable substances and potential ignition sources.

One of the most important components of technical fire protection equipment is primary fire extinguishing equipment.

Primary fire extinguishing means are intended to eliminate small fires and fires at an early stage by the personnel of the facility, without waiting for the arrival of emergency rescue units of the Ministry of Emergency Situations. Such means include fire extinguishers, fire equipment (fire buckets, boxes with sand, shovels, fire blankets, barrels with water, etc.) and fire tools (crowbars, axes, etc.).

The most effective primary fire extinguishing agent is a fire extinguisher. Portable fire extinguishers (carbon dioxide, powder, air-foam) with a body volume of 5 to 100 liters can be used as primary fire extinguishing means at facilities.

In Belarus, carbon dioxide and powder fire extinguishers are mostly used. In residential buildings, offices, archives and premises where computers, office equipment and other valuable equipment are located, carbon dioxide fire extinguishers are often used (fig. 7).

Fig. 7. Carbon dioxide fire extinguisher

Powder fire extinguishers are suitable for use in production, storage facilities, garages and cars. They are very effective in extinguishing flammable liquids and gases. Despite the widespread use of powder fire extinguishers, some of them (powder fire extinguishers with gas-generating devices) pose a serious danger to humans and are prohibited by the legislation of the Republic of Belarus.

There are general rules for using a fire extinguishers:

1. First, prepare the device for operation - break the seal and pull out the pin. The fire extinguisher is triggered when you press the lever.

2. To avoid fire from getting on you, stand on the side from which the wind is blowing. This will also help you avoid inhaling harmful substances that are present in the contents of the fire extinguisher.

3. The stream should be directed at the base of the burning surface, not at the flame itself. This rule does not include cases where the fire has started in a niche - the stream should be directed from top to bottom. As for the burning vertical surface, it should be extinguished from the bottom up.

4. If there are several fire extinguishers, it is better to use them all at once. To do this, you need to involve people nearby.

5. When finished, make sure that the flame has gone out completely and there are no more ignition sources.

When working with a fire extinguisher, it is important to remember the following:

  1. If a powder fire extinguisher is used in a closed and small space, it is necessary to ventilate the room immediately after stopping extinguishing, as the powder irritates the respiratory system.
  2. When using a carbon dioxide fire extinguisher, it is forbidden to hold the bell, as it cools down very much, which can lead to frostbite of the hands. Also, using a carbon dioxide fire extinguisher in closed rooms with a small volume is dangerous due to carbon dioxide poisoning, so after extinguishing the fire, you must leave the room and take measures to ventilate it.
  3. Carbon dioxide and powder fire extinguishers, after being activated, must not be turned into a horizontal position - they must be oriented vertically, otherwise their efficiency will be minimal (zero).

Lesson 5. First aid for electric shock, lightning, flame burns, frostbite, drowning

1. The effect of electric current on the human body. The sequence and content of measures to provide first aid

The dangerous and harmful effects of electric current on people are manifested in the form of electrical injuries . Electrical injuries are injuries caused by the impact of electric current on the body, which are conventionally divided into general (electric shock), local and mixed.

The concept of electric shock. Electric shock is understood as damage to living tissues of the body by an electric current flowing through it, manifested in involuntary convulsive contractions of various muscles of the body. In the case of electric shock, the entire body is at risk of damage due to the disruption of the normal functioning of its various organs and systems, including the heart, lungs, central nervous system, etc. 

Depending on the outcome of the injury, electric shocks can be conditionally divided into the following five degrees:
I - convulsive, barely perceptible muscle contraction;

II – convulsive contraction of muscles, accompanied by severe, barely bearable pain, without loss of consciousness;

III – convulsive contraction of muscles with loss of consciousness, but with preserved breathing and heart function;

IV – loss of consciousness and disruption of cardiac activity or breathing;
V – clinical death, i.e. absence of breathing and blood circulation.

Local damage caused by exposure to electric current. Typical local electrical injuries include electrical burns, electrical signs, skin metallization, mechanical damage, and electro-ophthalmia .

Electrical burns, which occur as a result of heat being released when current passes through the human body, are the most common electrical injury. Depending on the conditions of occurrence, there are two main types of burns: current (or contact) and arc.

An electric burn occurs when current passes directly through the human body as a result of contact with a live part. This type of burn is usually a skin burn at the point of contact of the body with the live part. An electric burn occurs when contacting an electrical installation with a relatively low voltage – no higher than 1000-2000 V and is usually characterized by I or II degree (reddening of the skin, formation of blisters); sometimes severe burns of III and IV degrees occur ( at voltages higher than 380 V) .

An arc burn is the result of the impact of an electric arc on the body, which has a high temperature (over 3500° C) and high energy. This burn usually occurs upon contact with a high-voltage electrical installation - over 1000 V and, as a rule, is severe - III or IV degree (necrosis of the entire thickness of the skin, charring of tissue, etc.). An electric arc can cause extensive burns of the body, burning of tissue to a great depth, charring and traceless burning of large areas of the body.

Electric sign – this is a specific skin lesion, expressed in necrosis of the affected areas. Electrical marks are clearly defined spots of gray or pale yellow color. Often the marks are round or oval in shape and 1-5 mm in size. The surface of the mark is dry and not inflamed. The affected area of skin hardens like a callus. As a rule, electrical marks are painless and their treatment ends successfully.

Metallization of the skin is the penetration of tiny metal particles into the skin under the influence of electric current. The affected area of the skin has a rough and hard surface, the color of which is determined by the color of the metal compounds that have penetrated the skin: green - upon contact with copper, gray - with aluminum, yellow-gray - with lead. The affected area experiences pain from the burn and the presence of foreign bodies. Over time, the diseased skin acquires a normal appearance and the painful sensations disappear. Only if the eyes are affected can the treatment be long and complicated, and in some cases, ineffective. Usually exposed parts of the body are affected – hands and face.

Mechanical damage occurs under the influence of current, involuntary convulsive contraction of muscles. This can lead to rupture of skin, blood vessels and nerve tissue. Mechanical damage occurs when working mainly in installations up to 1000 V with a relatively long stay of a person under voltage. Mechanical damage occurs quite rarely and requires long-term treatment. Such damage is always accompanied by electric shocks. Some of them are also accompanied by contact burns of the body. 

Electrophthalmia is a lesion of the outer membranes of the eyes due to exposure to ultraviolet radiation from an electric arc. Electrophthalmia develops 4-8 hours after ultraviolet irradiation. In this case, there is redness and inflammation of the skin and mucous membranes of the eyelids, lacrimation, purulent discharge from the eyes, eyelid spasms and partial blindness. The disease usually lasts for several days. In case of damage to the cornea, treatment is more complex and lengthy. The electric arc creates a strong light radiation (brighter than Sun ), therefore, for example, welders use special glasses with dark glass to protect the eyes.

Factors affecting the outcome of electric shock:

1. Passage of current through the human body. The path of current in the human body depends on which parts of the body the victim touches the live parts. The path of electric current through the human body is called a loop. The most dangerous type is a complete loop, which connects four limbs and affects all internal organs (Fig. 21). 

Fig. 21. Complete ( W -shaped) current flow loop

2. Resistance of human tissues. Different tissues of the body provide different resistance to current. Thus, bones, cartilage, ligaments and skin provide high resistance to current. Muscles and blood provide comparatively low resistance.

Methods of freeing a victim from the effects of electric current, personal safety measures. The first action that must be taken when a person is exposed to electric current is to turn off the electrical installation that is under voltage. However, in the conditions of large industrial enterprises, this is not always possible. Then it is necessary to cut or chop the wire or cable with an axe with a dry wooden handle, or to pull the victim away from the source of current. 

To separate the victim from live parts or wires, use dry clothing, a stick, a board or any other dry object that does not conduct electric current. The use of metal or wet objects for these purposes is not allowed.

To separate the victim from live parts, you can also grab his clothes (if they are dry and do not come away from the victim's body), while avoiding touching surrounding metal objects and body parts not covered by clothing.

When pulling the victim by the legs, do not touch his shoes or clothes without properly insulating your hands, as shoes and clothes may be damp and conduct electric current.

To isolate the hands, the person providing assistance, especially if it is necessary to touch the body of the victim who is not covered by clothing, should wrap his hands with a scarf, put a cloth cap on his hands, use rubberized material (raincoat) or just dry material.

If the victim touches a torn high-voltage line wire, and the wire is lying on the ground, then before approaching the victim, it is necessary to place a dry board, a bundle of dry clothes under his feet and separate the wire from the victim using a dry board or a stick. When separating the victim from live parts, it is recommended to act with one hand if possible.

If it is not possible to separate the victim from the live parts, then the wires should be cut or severed. You can use an axe if its handle is absolutely dry. You need to cut the wires (each one separately), placing the same board under them.

In addition, the wires (each one separately) can be cut with pliers or nippers with insulated handles; in extreme cases, you can use a tool with metal handles, but wrap them in dry woolen or rubberized material.

Failure to cut the wires individually may result in a short circuit. The resulting arc can cause severe burns to the body and eye damage to the person providing the aid.

If a person is exposed to voltage above 1000 V, such precautions are not sufficient. It is necessary to contact specialists who will immediately remove the voltage.

Sequence and content of first aid measures. First aid measures depend on the condition of the victim after being released from the effects of electric current.

If the victim is conscious but has previously fainted, he should be placed in a comfortable position (put something underneath him and cover him with some clothing) and ensure complete rest until the doctor arrives, continuously monitoring his breathing and pulse. It is prohibited to allow the victim to move, much less continue working, since the absence of symptoms after electric shock does not exclude the possibility of subsequent deterioration of the victim's condition. If it is not possible to quickly call a doctor, it is necessary to urgently deliver the victim to a medical facility, providing the necessary transport or stretcher for this.

If the victim is unconscious, but with stable breathing and pulse, he should be laid flat and comfortably, unbuttoned, create an influx of fresh air, give him ammonia to smell, spray his face with water and provide complete rest. If the victim is breathing poorly - very rarely and convulsively (like a dying person), he should be given artificial respiration and indirect (external) heart massage.

If the victim shows no signs of life (breathing and pulse), he cannot be considered dead, since death is often only apparent. In such a state, the victim will actually die if he is not given immediate first aid in the form of artificial respiration and external (indirect) cardiac massage. Artificial respiration should be performed continuously, both before and after the doctor arrives. The question of the advisability or futility of further artificial respiration is decided by the doctor.

2. Flame burns, sequence and content of first aid measures. Frostbite, first aid measures (by stages)

Flame, acting on tissue, causes moisture to evaporate and protein to coagulate. With prolonged exposure to it, the skin contracts, stretches, ­and breaks, forming cracks and ruptures resembling cut wounds.

During primary wound treatment, it is difficult to remove the remains of burnt clothing; unnoticed threads of fabric may subsequently serve as foci of infection. The organs of vision may be affected. Burns of the skin combined with burns of the mucous membranes of the upper respiratory tract are especially dangerous. Such combinations are possible if the victim breathes hot smoke and air. This usually occurs during a fire in a closed room. Burns of the skin and mucous membranes during a fire may be combined with carbon monoxide poisoning.

Sequence of first aid for flame burns:

1. Eliminate the source of the burn. If a person's clothes catch fire, you must immediately remove them or lay the victim on his back. You can also throw a blanket, coat, bag, or overcoat over the victim, that is, cut off the air supply to the fire. Flames on clothes can be extinguished with water or covered with sand. You cannot run in burning clothes, as the wind, fanning the flames, will increase and intensify the burn. It is necessary to remove the victim from the high-temperature zone.

2. Treatment of a burn wound. When treating a wound, it is necessary to remove clothing very carefully so as not to damage the tissue even more. Moreover, if the clothing has stuck to the skin, it must not be torn off! It is necessary to carefully cut off the non-stick part of the clothing along the edges, using sharp scissors for this.

For small first-degree (redness, pain) and second-degree (severe redness, severe pain, blisters) burns, apply a sterile bandage to the burned area of skin.

In case of severe and extensive burns (more than 10% of the body surface), the victim must be wrapped in a clean sheet or cloth without undressing him, covered warmly, given warm tea to drink and kept at rest until the doctor arrives.  

When providing assistance to the victim, to avoid infection, do not touch the burned areas of the skin with your hands or lubricate them with ointments, fats, oils, petroleum jelly, sprinkle with baking soda, starch, etc. Do not open blisters, remove mastic, rosin or other resinous substances that have stuck to the burned area, since by removing them, you can easily tear off the burned skin and thereby create favorable conditions for infection of the wound. 

First aid measures for frostbite. The first stage is before the affected body part begins to warm up.

You should immediately stop the exposure to low temperatures, take the victim into a warm room or car. The main task is to gradually restore blood circulation in the affected part of the body. You should remove tight shoes and clothing that squeeze the limb and disrupt blood flow. Shoes should be removed carefully so as not to damage frostbitten toes. If this cannot be done without effort, you need to rip the boot or shoe shaft seam with a knife.

It is impossible to allow the surface layers of the skin in the affected area to warm up quickly, since the deep layers warm up more slowly. Blood flow is poorly restored in them and the upper layers of the skin do not receive enough oxygen and nutrients. As a result of nutritional deficiencies, they die. In this regard, in case of frostbite, the use of hot baths, hot air, heating pads, and hot water bottles is strictly contraindicated. Rubbing the affected areas of the body with snow is prohibited. Massage is not recommended.

A dry heat-insulating bandage, such as cotton-gauze, is applied to the frostbitten area. If this is not available, woolen fabric can be used (over cotton). When noticeable warmth appears, the bandage is removed. The affected limb must be kept at rest. To replenish the body's warmth, the victim is given hot sweet tea.

The second stage is after warming up the affected part of the body. A dry sterile bandage or one ironed with a hot iron is applied to the affected area of the body. In case of severe pain, a painkiller is given. After first aid has been provided, the victim should be taken to a medical facility as quickly as possible. During transportation, the affected limb should be immobilized (immobilized).

3. Drowning. Methods of extracting a drowning person from the water, personal safety measures. Types of drowning, features of first aid in case of true drowning

Drowning is an acute pathological condition that occurs as a result of suffocation due to accidental or intentional immersion in water.

Types of drowning. Syncopal drowning. This type of drowning is typical for the vast majority of drowned people during disasters . Syncopal drowning is characterized by primary reflex cardiac and respiratory arrest caused by even a small amount of water entering the upper respiratory tract.

A similar mechanism of development is found in the so-called “ice shock” or immersion syndrome, which develops as a result of reflex cardiac arrest during sudden immersion in cold water.

Asphyxial (dry) drowning. It can be caused by overheating in the sun, alcohol consumption, heart attack, etc. Breathing stops due to a spasm of the glottis when the first portions of water enter.

"Death on the Water". The cause of the victim's terminal condition in the water is not related to the liquid entering his respiratory tract, but to another reason (myocardial infarction, acute cerebrovascular accident, or a suddenly developing epileptic seizure).

Aspiration or "wet" drowning (or otherwise true) occurs when water enters the victim's respiratory tract and fills the lungs (75-95% of cases). Most often occurs in children and adults who cannot swim, and in people who are intoxicated.

Signs of true drowning:

  • The skin of the face and neck has a bluish tint.
  • Swelling of the vessels of the neck.
  • Profuse foamy discharge from the mouth and nose.

Methods of extracting a drowning person from water, personal safety measures. When rescuing a drowning person on the surface of the water, special care should be taken. A victim in a state of panic fear is capable of performing "dead" grips. As a result, the number of victims may increase. If there is a real threat to one's own life, it is recommended to extract the drowning person using improvised means (a stick, rope, belt), stretched from a safe distance.

Features of first aid in case of true drowning. The first thing that needs to be done is throw the drowned person face down over the rescuer's knee. The main thing is to insert two fingers into the victim's mouth as quickly as possible and remove the contents of the oral cavity with a circular motion (Fig. 22). After cleaning the oral cavity, it is necessary to press sharply on the root of the tongue to provoke a gag reflex and stimulate breathing. If the gag reflex is preserved and food remains are visible in the water pouring out of the mouth, this indicates that the person is alive. This will be confirmed by the appearance of a cough. Next, you should remove water from the stomach and lungs as thoroughly as possible for 5-10 minutes, still pressing on the base of the tongue in a face-down position. At the same time, you can pat the victim on the back with your palm, and also intensively squeeze the chest from the sides several times while inhaling.

After removing water from the upper respiratory tract, lungs and stomach, it is necessary to lay the victim on his side and call an ambulance.

Fig. 22. First aid for true drowning

If there is no gag reflex when pressing on the tongue, there are no food remnants in the flowing water, no coughing or breathing movements are observed, then cardiopulmonary resuscitation should be started immediately. To do this, the victim should be laid on his back and indirect cardiac massage should be started and artificial respiration , which every 3-4 minutes should be alternated with the removal of the contents of the mouth and nose, turning the person onto his stomach.

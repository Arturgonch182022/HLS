Practical work No. 1

Familiarisation with portable dosimeters and training in their use

Purpose of work

To study the rules of work with dosimeters, their preparation for work and techniques of use.

Theory of work

Radioactivity is a phenomenon in which the nuclei of one chemical element spontaneously transform into nuclei of another element or isotopes of the same element. The process is accompanied by emission of particles and electromagnetic radiation.

Dosimetry is the determination of the dose of radioactive radiation absorbed by an object.

Radioactive radiation is not directly perceived by our senses. But it can be detected and measured indirectly.

Detection methods are based on the fact that radiation leaves a trace or is trapped in the matter through which it passes. The special detectors used today have different physical bases (gas, scintillation, semiconductor counters), but they use the same principle: they translate photons, electrons or alpha particles of radiation into an electrical signal to calculate the number of decays or in other words the number of becquerels.

Although 1 becquerel is an extremely small amount of radioactivity, the measuring instruments available to mankind are in most cases sensitive enough to detect radioactivity. Radioactivity can be measured either in the laboratory or with portable apparatus designed to record a specific type of radiation.

The three units of radioactivity are the becquerel, the gray and the sievert, in which radioactivity, its energy and exposure are measured, respectively (Table 1).

The activity in becquerels (Bq) is equal to the number of atoms decaying per second (1 Bq corresponds to the decay of one atom per second). Previously, the Curie unit was used to indicate the number of decays - corresponding to thirty-seven billion decays per second, named in honour of the discoverers of radium - Pierre and Marie Curie.

Gray (Gy) is a unit of measurement of the amount of energy released in a substance when exposed to radiation. 1 Gy corresponds to the fact that a substance has received one joule of energy per kilogramme of mass and determines the absorbed dose. Previously, the unit "rad" was used.

Sievert (Sv) is a unit of biological impact on the organism depending on the type of radiation. 1 sievert is the amount of energy absorbed by a kilogram of biological tissue, equal in effect to the absorbed dose of gamma radiation of 1 Gy. The equivalent dose, which characterises the biological effect of exposing an organism to ionising radiation, is measured in sieverts. Previously, the unit of Behr was used, which is 1 hundredth of a sievert. The annual average dose to the Earth's inhabitants from all sources is estimated at 2400 mR = 2.4 mSv.

Radiation monitoring is constantly carried out in Belarus. Information from the posts located throughout the country is updated once a day. At 12 of them, located near the Ignalina, Chernobyl, Rovno and Smolensk NPPs, measurements are taken every three hours. Data on the radiation background level in Belarus are also provided on the website of the Republican Hydrometeorological Centre

There are several types of dosimeters, some detect radiation in any objects, others detect a sudden change around and will tell you when this indicator will increase the norm, and there are those that quickly find radioactive objects at a great distance.

Household instruments usually have light and/or sound indication and a display for counting measurements. The size and design range from a wristband to a "pocket" version. Continuous operation time from a single battery is from a few hours to several months.

Gas-discharge indicators of ionising radiation based on the effect of avalanche breakdown of ionised space at field strength close to the critical field strength but not exceeding it are used as a radiation recording element in dosimeters. For this purpose, the field strength in the interelectrode space of the Geiger-Muller counter is maintained in the saturation state.

When ionising radiation enters the sensor space, forced ionisation (appearance of free charge carriers) occurs under its influence and avalanche breakdown occurs in the charged electric field along the track. Let us consider the main types of dosimeters.

Dosimeter alarm. It is a device that will immediately notice fluctuations of radiation around you and, if they increase and exceed the norm, will inform you about it with an alarm.

An indicator and a meter are roughly similar. Both types can tell you if there is radioactivity in a thing. But meters are very accurate and can even detect radiation in the soil, whether it is there or not.

Finder. The device does not report changes in radioactive indicators around, but can find any anomaly, ore and other things. More useful for serious research and experiments.

Task 2: Measurement of natural background radiation.

The task is performed using the radioactivity indicator RKSB -104 (or another model provided by the teacher).

Familiarise yourself with the instructions supplied with the appliance.

Radioactivity indicator RKSB -104 is designed to detect and assess the level of ionising radiation. It performs the functions of a dosimeter and radiometer to monitor the radiation situation on the ground, in living and working premises.

The device RKSB-104 evaluates the radiation situation by the dose rate taking into account X-rays with the help of two Geiger-Muller counters within 25-30 seconds and induces readings in µSv/h or µR/h on the liquid crystal display. The registration of each particle is accompanied by an audible signal, which makes it possible to use this instrument when searching for areas contaminated with radioactive substances.
